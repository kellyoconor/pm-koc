# GTM Strategy

## Overview

Create a comprehensive go-to-market strategy for a product launch. Covers market positioning, channel selection, messaging development, launch execution, and measurement. Incorporates strategies from experienced product leaders on PR, launch marketing, and distribution.

## When to Use

- Planning a product launch (new product, major feature, or new market)
- Creating a GTM plan from scratch
- Defining a launch strategy for a new market
- Developing product-to-market fit strategy
- Coordinating a go-to-market campaign
- Preparing a product go-live roadmap

## Core Principles

### Launch tiers: match effort to impact
Most features deserve a silent or soft launch. Save the big-bang for genuinely new products or major pivots. Linear's principle: launch early, launch often, launch multiple times. Maximum 2-3 big-bang launches per year.

### Separate soft launch from hard launch
Decouple the technical release (soft launch) from the marketing event (hard launch). Soft launch first to gather functional videos and testimonials. Use the period between launches to plan a high-impact campaign based on the final product (Janna Bastow).

### Pitch press as an exclusive, not a broad embargo
For early-stage startups, offer the story exclusively to one outlet at a time. Identify a single target outlet that covers companies at your stage (Arielle Jackson).

### Find the "sizzle" feature
Identify a visually impressive or shareable feature that generates buzz, even if it isn't the primary use case. Satellite imagery wasn't the most important part of Google Maps, but it created a viral moment (Bret Taylor).

### Use lightning strikes, not peanut butter
Concentrate marketing efforts into 1-2 massive events per year rather than spreading budget thinly over time. Be unavoidable to your target audience for a short window (Christopher Lochhead).

### Distribute in concentric circles
Start from your own desk: first get clear on your message, then co-founders, executives, employees, investors, power users, and outward. Ensure each inner circle is fully aligned before moving to the next (Lulu Cheng Meservey).

### PR is for credibility, not leads
Press coverage builds social proof for recruiting and sales outreach. Don't rely on it for direct user acquisition (Zoelle Egner).

### Build a cadence of launches
Instead of one big launch, plan a series of launches every few months to re-engage communities and maintain presence (Zoelle Egner).

## How It Works

### Step 1: Gather Research Data

Load and analyze early research about your product and target market:
- Product description and key features
- Target market segment details
- Market research or validation data
- Competitive landscape information
- Customer interviews or survey data

### Step 2: Define Marketing Channels

Evaluate which channels best reach your target audience:
- **Digital marketing**: Paid search, social media, display advertising
- **Content and inbound**: Blog, SEO, thought leadership
- **Sales and outbound**: Direct outreach, partnerships, field events
- **Community and grassroots**: Conferences, Slack groups, Reddit, Discord
- **Product-led and viral**: In-product sharing, referral mechanics
- **PR and media**: Press exclusives, influencer seeding, Product Hunt

Seed product to the right influencers -- find the intersection of people who will be obsessed with the product AND have a large following among your target audience (Lulu Cheng Meservey).

### Step 3: Develop Messaging

Create audience-specific messaging that resonates:
- Core value proposition for target segment
- Key differentiators and competitive advantages
- Pain point validation and solution mapping
- Proof points and social proof strategies
- Channel-specific messaging variations
- The "sizzle" feature for shareability

### Step 4: Define Success Metrics

Establish measurable KPIs before launch (not after):
- **Awareness**: Impressions, reach, brand recall
- **Engagement**: CTR, cost per engagement, time on site
- **Conversion**: Signups, demos requested, trials started
- **Revenue**: MRR, customer acquisition cost, lifetime value
- **Market**: Market share, segment penetration

### Step 5: Create Launch Plan

Build a phased launch timeline with appropriate tier:

**Pre-launch (6 weeks out for press)**:
- Messaging finalized, website ready for traffic
- Internal teams briefed on messaging
- Press outreach / influencer seeding begun
- Blog post, demo video, social posts drafted

**Launch day**:
- Coordinated announcements and activities
- Support team staffed and briefed with FAQ
- One person on point for first 24 hours

**Post-launch momentum**:
- Day 1: Watch metrics, fix breakages, respond to all feedback
- Day 3: First retro -- what's working, what's not, what surprised you
- Week 1: Decide whether to iterate, expand, or pull back
- Week 2-4: Write up learnings, update roadmap based on real data
- Use press coverage as marketing fuel -- the "As seen in" logo may be more valuable than direct traffic

## Input Format

Use $ARGUMENTS to pass:
- Product name and description
- Target market segment
- Research data or file path
- Launch timeline and constraints
- Budget or resource limitations

## Output

A structured GTM strategy document including:
- Recommended launch tier (silent, soft, or big-bang) with justification
- Marketing channels with justification and priority
- Channel-specific messaging and positioning
- Launch timeline with key milestones
- KPI targets and measurement framework
- Risk mitigation and rollback strategies
- 90-day execution roadmap

## Guidelines

- NEVER launch without a rollback plan. If you can't revert, you're not ready.
- ALWAYS define success metrics BEFORE launch, not after. Post-hoc metrics are storytelling.
- NEVER launch on a Friday. Launch Monday-Wednesday for monitoring time.
- ALWAYS have one person on point for the first 24 hours.
- Start with your most confident customer segment
- Focus on a few channels excellently rather than many channels poorly
- Establish baseline metrics before launch to measure impact
- Plan for feedback loops and optimization
- Be flexible on launch dates to secure quality press coverage

## Tips

- Start PR prep six weeks out: blog post, media training, outreach, investor quotes (Emilie Gerber)
- For platform-based launches (Product Hunt, podcasts), concentrate activity in a short window to trigger algorithmic boost (Chris Hutchins)
- Don't launch too many things at once -- introduce radical innovations one at a time
- Funding is a news hook, not the story. Combine funding news with product or customer momentum (Arielle Jackson)
