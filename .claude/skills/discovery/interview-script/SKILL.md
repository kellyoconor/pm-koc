## Customer Interview Script

Create a structured interview script that surfaces real insights, not just opinions. Follows The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick) principles and YC's Five Questions (Eric Migicovsky) framework — ask about their life, not your idea.

### Domain Context

Customer interviews are one source in **Stage 1 (Explore)** of continuous discovery. Other sources: stakeholder interviews, usage analytics, data analytics, surveys, market trends, SEO/SEM analysis. The PM needs direct access to users, stakeholders, engineers, and designers — "without proxies." The **Product Trio** (PM + Designer + Engineer — Teresa Torres) should work together on discovery, not the PM alone.

### Context

You are preparing a customer interview script for research on **$ARGUMENTS**.

If the user provides files (personas, hypothesis lists, product briefs, or previous interview notes), read them first.

### Instructions

1. **Clarify research objectives**:
   - What specific questions does the team need answered?
   - What decisions will this research inform?
   - What assumptions need validation?
   - What is the product stage? (new idea, existing product, churn investigation, feature prioritization)

2. **Determine interview methodology** based on the research goal:
   - **Problem validation (Mom Test style)**: Confirm a problem exists and is painful enough to solve
   - **Jobs-to-be-Done discovery**: Understand what customers are trying to accomplish and why current solutions fail
   - **Retention/churn investigation**: Figure out why customers leave or don't activate
   - **Switch interviews**: Interview customers who recently switched from a competitor or alternative
   - **Timeline/journey mapping**: Walk through their entire experience chronologically

3. **Recruit the right participants**:
   - Only interview people who have taken action — Bob Moesta: "Ignore 'bitching' (complaining) — look for 'switching' (actual behavior change)"
   - Interview people who experience the problem regularly and recently
   - Consider interviewing non-users — Mihika Kapoor: "The most insightful conversations are with non-users"
   - Aim for 7-14 interviews — Shaun Clowes: "Between 7-14 interviews, you stop learning new things"
   - Expect 90% rejection when recruiting — Gustaf Alstromer: "You need to reach 10 to find 1"
   - Recruiting tip: Email 20-30 people to get 5-10 interviews (33% response rate is typical)

4. **Create the interview script** with these sections:

   ### Opening (3-5 min)
   - Introduce yourself and the purpose (learning, not selling)
   - Set expectations: "There are no right or wrong answers. We're here to learn from your experience."
   - "This isn't a sales call — I'm here to learn from your experience. Critical feedback is most helpful."
   - Ask permission to record (if applicable)
   - Confirm time available

   ### Warm-Up: Context & Background (5 min)
   - "Tell me about your role and what a typical day/week looks like."
   - "How long have you been doing [activity related to the product area]?"
   - Goal: Build rapport and understand their context

   ### Core Exploration: The Five Questions Backbone (15-25 min)

   Use YC's Five Questions (Eric Migicovsky) as the backbone. They work in order — each digs deeper:

   **1. "What's the hardest part about [doing the thing you're exploring]?"**
   Opens with their pain, not your solution.

   **2. "Tell me about the last time you encountered that problem."**
   Forces specifics. Past behavior > hypothetical opinions.

   **3. "Why was that hard?"**
   Uncovers root cause. Their first answer is usually surface-level.

   **4. "What, if anything, have you done to try to solve this?"**
   Existing workarounds = real pain. No workarounds = not painful enough.

   **5. "What don't you love about the solutions you've tried?"**
   Reveals gaps in existing alternatives. This is your opportunity space.

   ### Follow-Up Probes (Gustaf Alstromer)

   After the Five Questions, probe for signal strength:
   - **Frequency:** "How often does this come up?" (Daily = strong signal)
   - **Workarounds:** "Show me how you do it today." (Screenshots, spreadsheets, hacks = gold)
   - **Willingness to pay:** "Have you looked for or paid for a solution?" (Spending = strongest signal)
   - **Emotional weight:** Watch for energy shifts — frustration, sighing, storytelling. That's where the real pain lives. Nan Yu: "My goal is to feel bad the same way customers feel bad."

   ### JTBD Deep-Dive (optional, 5-10 min)

   If exploring switching behavior, use Forces of Progress:
   - **Push:** "What was frustrating about how you were doing it before?"
   - **Pull:** "What attracted you to the new way?"
   - **Anxiety:** "What worried you about switching?"
   - **Habit:** "What kept you doing it the old way for so long?"

   ### Probing Techniques
   Use these when you hit an interesting thread:
   - **"Tell me more about that"** — opens up any topic
   - **"Why?"** (asked gently, 2-3 times) — gets to root causes
   - **"Can you give me a specific example?"** — moves from opinions to facts
   - **"What happened next?"** — follows the story
   - **"How did that make you feel?"** — captures emotional intensity
   - **Silence** — Jeff Weinstein: "Use silence to let them open up"

   ### The Mom Test Rules
   These rules override all default interviewing instincts:
   - Ask about **their life**, not your idea
   - Ask about **the past**, not the future ("Would you use X?" is useless)
   - NEVER ask "Would you use this?" or "What would you pay?" — hypothetical questions get hypothetical answers
   - **Talk less, listen more** — aim for 80/20 split
   - **Never pitch** during the interview. The moment you pitch, you stop learning.
   - Deflect compliments immediately. "That sounds great!" -> "Thanks — but tell me more about how you currently handle this."
   - Anchor fluff to specifics. "I always struggle with..." -> "When was the last time? Walk me through it."
   - Look for **strong emotions** — they signal real pain or delight
   - **Compliments are noise** — "That sounds cool!" tells you nothing

   ### Wrap-Up (3-5 min)
   - Summarize: "Just to recap, I heard that [key insights]. Did I get that right?"
   - "Is there anything I didn't ask that you think is important?"
   - "Who else should I talk to about this?" — your best interviewees come from referrals
   - Thank them for their time
   - Share next steps (if any)

5. **Seek advancement, not enthusiasm.** Advancement = they give you time, reputation, or money (intro to a colleague, agree to a pilot, share their data). Enthusiasm = "sounds cool!" (worthless).

6. **Customize the script**: Adapt questions to the specific product area, persona, and research objectives. Add or remove sections based on the interview length available.

7. **Include a note-taking template**:
   ```
   Participant: [Name / ID]
   Date: [Date]
   Key Jobs: [What they're trying to accomplish]
   Current Solution: [What they use today]
   Biggest Pain: [Their #1 frustration]
   Desired Outcome: [What success looks like]
   Workarounds: [What they've tried — hacks, tools, manual processes]
   Willingness to Pay: [How much they invest / would invest]
   Emotional Intensity: [Where energy shifted — frustration, excitement]
   Surprise Finding: [Something unexpected]
   Referrals: [Who else to talk to]
   Follow-up: [Next steps]
   ```

Save as markdown. Include both the script and the note-taking template.

### Biases to Avoid

1. **Confirmation bias:** Don't ask "Don't you think X is a problem?" -> Ask "Tell me about your experience with X."
2. **Leading questions:** Don't ask "Would you use this?" -> Ask "What have you tried? Why did it work/fail?"
3. **Hypothetical questions:** Don't ask "If we built Y, would you pay?" -> Ask "What do you currently pay for? Why?"
4. **Pitching disguised as research:** Don't say "We're building Z to solve X" -> Say "I'm researching X. Tell me about your experience."
5. **Yes/no questions:** Don't ask "Is invoicing hard?" -> Ask "Walk me through your invoicing process."

### Success Criteria

You'll know these interviews are successful if:
- You hear **specific stories**, not generic complaints — "Last Tuesday, I spent 3 hours..." vs. "Invoicing is annoying"
- You uncover **past behavior**, not hypothetical wishes — "I tried Zapier but quit after 2 weeks" vs. "I'd probably use automation"
- You identify **patterns across 3+ interviews** — Same pain points emerge independently
- You're **surprised by something** — If everything confirms your assumptions, you're asking leading questions
- You can **quote customers verbatim** — Actual language = authentic insights
- You get **advancement** — Referrals, intros, pilot agreements

### Interview Logistics

- 45-60 minutes per interview (30-40 min conversation + buffer)
- Record if possible (with consent), or take detailed notes on exact quotes, not your interpretation
- Schedule 2-3 per day max (you need time to synthesize)
- After each interview, write key insights immediately (memory fades fast)
- After 5 interviews, look for patterns (common pains, jobs, workarounds)
- Stop interviewing when you stop hearing new things (usually 5-8 for a focused problem space)
- Co-create with lighthouse users: Tanguy Crusson: "Work with 10 'lighthouse' users over months. Put them in Slack with your team."
