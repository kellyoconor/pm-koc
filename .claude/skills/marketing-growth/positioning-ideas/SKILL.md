# Product Positioning

Develop product positioning that articulates who you serve, what need you address, how you're categorized, what benefit you deliver, and how you differ from alternatives. Combines competitive landscape analysis with structured positioning frameworks to produce actionable, testable positioning statements.

## When to Use

- Defining positioning for a new product or major pivot
- Differentiating from specific competitors in your messaging
- Aligning stakeholders (product, marketing, sales) on strategic positioning
- Identifying market positioning gaps and unclaimed territory
- Preparing for launch, PRDs, sales collateral, or press releases
- Repositioning an existing product when messaging feels fuzzy or generic
- Triggers: positioning, brand positioning, differentiation, how to position, positioning statement, value proposition

## Core Principles

### Positioning dictates everything
Arielle Jackson: "I really believe that positioning dictates so much of your marketing and should always be the first thing you do." Positioning is foundational -- it should precede product development and all other marketing activities.

### Start from competitive alternatives, not features
April Dunford's Obviously Awesome framework works because it's grounded in reality: what alternatives exist, what you do differently, and who cares most about that difference. Never position in a vacuum.

### Weak positioning creates pipeline friction
April Dunford: "Weak positioning hurts you in the early stages of pipeline in that people don't really get what you are, so they're not responding to your marketing the way they should." Monitor for customers asking sales reps to "back up and start over" during pitches -- this signals positioning problems.

### Communication is defined by the receiver
Gina Gotthilf: "Communication isn't about being able to convey a message, it's about being able to convey a message in a way that the listener receives it, and understands it, and remembers it."

### Differentiation requires sacrifice
April Dunford: "You can't be everything to everyone." True positioning requires explicitly deciding who you're NOT for and what you're NOT offering.

### Start with the struggling moment
Bob Moesta: "A struggling moment causes demand." Great positioning starts with understanding the specific context where users feel stuck, not with product features.

## Prompt

You are an experienced brand strategist with expertise in competitive positioning, market differentiation, and brand strategy.

Given the following product and market context: $ARGUMENTS

Follow these steps:

**Step 1: Competitive Alternatives Analysis (April Dunford)**
What would your best customers do if you didn't exist? List real alternatives:
- Direct competitors (name the top 3-5)
- Indirect solutions (spreadsheets, hiring someone, different tools)
- Do nothing / status quo
- For each, note: their primary positioning angle, target audience, key differentiators, and positioning gaps they leave open

Be honest. If "do nothing" is the primary alternative, that tells you a lot about urgency.

**Step 2: Unique Attributes**
What do you have that the alternatives don't? List concrete, verifiable capabilities -- not marketing spin.
- "Real-time collaboration on specs" (verifiable)
- NOT "best-in-class experience" (unverifiable)

**Step 3: Value Translation**
What do those unique attributes ENABLE for the customer? Translate features into outcomes.
- Attribute: "Real-time cursors and inline comments"
- Value: "Get feedback from your team in minutes instead of waiting for a meeting"

**Step 4: Best-Fit Customers**
Who cares MOST about the value you deliver? Define them tightly:
- "Remote teams of 5-15 who collaborate daily but drown in Slack threads and lost Google Docs"
- NOT "product managers" (too broad)

Best-fit customers have: the problem acutely, tried alternatives, have budget/authority, and get the most value from your unique attributes.

**Step 5: Market Category**
What market do you position in so that your value is obvious? This is the LAST decision, not the first.
- **Existing category:** "We're a [known category] that [key differentiator]." Works when the category is well-understood.
- **Subcategory:** "We're [modifier] + [existing category]." Works when you serve a specific segment better.
- **New category:** Create a new name. Only works if you have the budget and patience to educate the market.

**Step 6: Positioning Statements**
For each positioning approach, provide:

**Geoffrey Moore Value Proposition:**
- **For** [specific target customer/persona]
- **that need** [underserved need -- focus on pains, gains, JTBD]
- [product name]
- **is a** [product category]
- **that** [benefit statement -- focus on outcomes, not features]

**Differentiation Statement:**
- **Unlike** [primary competitor or competitive alternative]
- [product name]
- **provides** [unique differentiation -- outcomes, not features]

**One-Sentence Summary:**
[Product name] is a [category] for [target] that [benefit], unlike [competitor] which [limitation].

**Step 7: Stress-Test the Positioning**
For each positioning statement, evaluate:
1. Would a customer recognize themselves in the "For [target]" clause?
2. Is the need defensible with research, interviews, or data?
3. Does the category help or hurt? Does it anchor you against the right competitors?
4. Is differentiation believable and provable with a demo, case study, or data?
5. Does this guide decisions? If someone asked "Should we build feature X?" would this positioning help answer it?
6. Could a competitor copy this differentiation in 6 months? If yes, it's not durable.

## Anti-Patterns to Flag

- **"For Everyone"**: "For businesses that want to grow" -- no one feels it's for them. Pick the first segment. Expand later.
- **Feature Creep in Benefit**: "That provides AI, automation, analytics, and integrations" -- lead with the outcome, not a feature list.
- **Imaginary Competitor**: "Unlike outdated legacy systems" -- name the actual competitor or substitute behavior.
- **Differentiation Without Proof**: "Provides revolutionary AI" -- make it falsifiable: "10x faster query performance on datasets under 1TB."
- **Category Confusion**: "Is a next-generation platform for digital transformation" -- buyers need a mental shelf. No shelf = no sale.
- **Positioning by Adjectives**: Using words like "powerful" or "easy-to-use" that every competitor also claims.
- **Company-Centric Language**: Using internal jargon that means nothing to prospects.

## Discovery Questions (When Context is Sparse)

Ask these to gather positioning inputs:

- "When a customer switches to your product, what are they switching FROM? What were they doing before?"
- "If your product disappeared tomorrow, what would your best customers miss most?"
- "What do your happiest customers say when they recommend you to a friend?"
- "Who is specifically NOT a good fit for your product, and why?"
- "What competitive alternative are you explicitly positioning against?"
- "Can everyone in your company describe what you do consistently?"

## Tips for Best Results

- Provide detailed target audience profiles and their pain points
- Share your product's unique capabilities and verifiable differentiators
- Mention current positioning (if any) and what's working or not working
- Include competitor positioning and messaging (paste website copy if available)
- Describe what market segment or niche you want to own
- Share customer testimonials, case studies, or win/loss analysis
- Provide sales objections or competitive intelligence
